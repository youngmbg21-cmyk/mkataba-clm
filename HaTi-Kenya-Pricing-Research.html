01Executive summary

HaTi should price in Kenyan shillings, bill annually, anchor to the value of a contract disaster avoided, and collect through M-Pesa and bank transfer — not USD, not monthly cards. The single biggest risk is not overpricing; it is under-pricing a genuinely differentiated local CLM out of instinct. The imported alternatives that Kenyan buyers would otherwise consider cost 3× to 50× more and quote in a currency that has become a liability.

The recommended shape is a free wedge + three shilling-priced tiers + a productized Advice Desk. Concrete numbers are built up in §06; the headline is below.

02What we are pricing

HaTi is a Contract Lifecycle Management (CLM) platform built for the Kenyan market — templates tuned to Kenyan practice, sealed SHA-256 e-signatures with an audit trail, a no-login counterparty portal, AI metadata extraction and playbook review, renewal/obligation tracking, approvals and multi-signer signing, and a portfolio-intelligence graph. It runs as a shared, multi-user workspace.

Critically, HaTi is two revenue engines in one product, which shapes the entire pricing model:

1 · The CLM software

Recurring, high-margin subscription. This is where the predictable ARR and the valuation multiple live. It should carry the recurring economics.

2 · The Advice Desk

A legal-services pipeline already priced at KES 7,500–10,500/hour (review, drafting, advice, negotiation, compliance; +25% for priority). Variable, trust-building, and the natural ROI proof for the software.

Why this matters for pricing

This product + services shape is the fastest-growing structure in legaltech (see §05). The strategic move is to let the software carry recurring revenue and bundle-then-meter the services — not the reverse. Pure hourly billing is the shrinking part of the market; recurring software is the part that compounds.

03Kenya market dynamics that dictate the model

Four local realities should decide how HaTi charges before we argue about how much. Each carries a direct pricing instruction.

3.1 · Currency & willingness-to-pay: shillings win

Kenyan buyers treat USD pricing as a friction and a risk. Local commentary consistently lists KES pricing — alongside M-Pesa and KRA/eTIMS compliance — as a core reason local tools beat QuickBooks/Zoho. The evidence base for regional pricing is strong: showing prices in local currency lifts conversion ~25%, fully localized pricing yields ~30% more revenue than straight FX conversion, and Stripe reported a 45% jump in emerging-market acquisition after regional pricing. Buyers shown a foreign currency feel they are paying up to 50% more than they actually are.

The cautionary tale is FX shock: the shilling ran from ~140 to ~163 per USD through 2023 before appreciating and stabilising at ~127–130 from late-2024 through mid-2026. A USD-priced tool got ~15% more expensive in shillings inside a year with zero change by the vendor. Inflation, by contrast, is tame (~4.3–4.6%).

Instruction

Publish fixed KES prices, not FX-linked ones. Keep USD as a secondary reference only. If any cost line is USD (cloud hosting), absorb the FX risk with an annual-repricing clause rather than exposing the buyer to spot rates.

3.2 · Payments & billing: annual, on rails that actually clear

Kenya is a mobile-money economy — 91% mobile-money penetration by mid-2025, M-Pesa clearing 60M+ transactions a day — while cards remain a minor rail (KES 297B in 2025, ~62M transactions). The consequence that matters for SaaS: recurring card billing is unreliable in Kenya (KES cards frequently fail card-on-file/renewal checks), so monthly auto-charge subscriptions churn on payment failure, not on dissatisfaction. Local SaaS leaders (Sheriasoft, Kwara) respond by billing annually or multi-period and collecting via M-Pesa/invoice.

Instruction

Default to annual billing. Collect via M-Pesa Paybill / Pesapal for SMEs and bank transfer + invoice for mid-market and enterprise. Offer M-Pesa standing orders (now supported) for those who want to spread payments, but make the headline price an annual one. This simultaneously fixes FX exposure, card-churn, and cash flow.

3.3 · Tax & regulation: cheap compliance, real trust asset

  • 16% VAT applies to SaaS from the first transaction — build it into pricing (VAT-inclusive display for SMEs; VAT-exclusive + line item for registered businesses that reclaim it).
  • Being a resident vendor is an edge. Foreign competitors now carry a ~19% combined load (16% VAT + ~3% Significant Economic Presence tax, which replaced the old 1.5% DST and lost its threshold in 2025). A local CLM pays normal corporate tax, not SEP — so imported tools are structurally more expensive to the buyer than their sticker suggests.
  • ODPC (Data Protection) registration is mandatory but trivial — KES 4,000–40,000 by tier, renewed every 24 months. For a product that holds contract data, this is a sales asset: lead with local data-protection compliance as a trust differentiator against foreign clouds.

3.4 · Market size & the real buyer

Kenya has ~7.4M MSMEs but only ~1.56M formal/licensed businesses — the realistic universe. CLM specifically is bought by the slice with a legal, procurement or finance function that manages contract volume: mid-market and large enterprises, plus law firms and in-house legal departments. The sample portfolio HaTi ships (a diversified FMCG with six value streams) is the archetype buyer. There is no published count of Kenyan firms with in-house legal/procurement — a TAM gap flagged for the research plan.

Data flag

No Kenya-specific B2B software willingness-to-pay survey exists; the annual-billing and WTP conclusions above are inferred from vendor pricing pages and payment-infrastructure constraints, not measured. The formal-SME count rests on the 2016 KNBS survey (2024 update pending). These are exactly the gaps §07 is designed to close.

The competitive read

You are not competing with Ironclad on price — you are competing on currency, locality, and reachability. Global CLM is either unaffordable, USD-denominated, or gated behind a sales team that won't call back a Nairobi mid-market firm. Global e-sign tools are cheap but are not CLM. Local legal software is affordable but is practice management, not contract lifecycle. HaTi's white space is "real CLM depth, local price, shillings, self-serve reachable."

05Directional lessons from SaaS worth learning from

Five patterns from businesses that solved emerging-market monetization — each with a concrete "apply it like this".

5.1 · Paystack — land narrow, expand on the same account

Paystack entered with a single wedge (a payments API), then layered Collections, Disbursements, Identity, Reporting and Commerce onto the same installed base — growing revenue per account without re-acquiring the customer (acquired by Stripe for $200M+). The African fintech norm is transaction/take-rate monetization precisely because it removes the upfront-cash barrier.

Apply it

Use a usage metric — active contracts processed — as one pricing dimension so a 3-lawyer firm enters cheap and the bill grows as they put more paper through HaTi. Land on the CLM wedge, then expand into playbooks, analytics, managed workflows and Advice Desk hours on the same account. Do not copy thin-margin volume plays (Twiga, Wasoko, MarketForce all stalled on them) — attach the high-margin Advice Desk as the profit engine, the way fintechs attached credit.

5.2 · Canva, GitHub, AWS — local currency is a weapon, not a courtesy

Global players now localize price level and currency for emerging markets: Canva Pro is ~54% cheaper in India (PPP-driven), GitHub gives developing-economy discounts, and AWS began billing in Naira and Egyptian Pound in 2025 — the biggest infra vendor conceding USD invoicing was a barrier. HubSpot offers local INR plans.

Apply it

Treat KES-native pricing as offence. It is one of the few structural advantages a local startup holds over Ironclad/DocuSign, who now feel 200%+ more expensive after FX moves. If HaTi ever expands to Nigeria/Ghana/SA, set fixed per-market local prices, never live FX conversion.

5.3 · Hybrid go-to-market beats pure self-serve for legal buyers

Freemium free-to-paid converts at just 2–5%; free trials at 10–25%; and legal/enterprise deals run 60–180 days on trust built "through conversations, not clicks". Yet the winners are hybrid: 67% net revenue retention for hybrid vs 58% for pure PLG (HubSpot, Notion, Figma, Zoom all run hybrid).

Apply it

Run a product-led wedge + sales-assisted close: a genuinely useful free tier gets HaTi in the door and demonstrated; a human closes. In a low-trust, long-cycle market, prefer a paid pilot over an open free trial — a nominal fee filters for real intent and creates a budget line. The Advice Desk is your built-in trust-builder that de-risks the software sale.

5.4 · The ALSP model — productize the hours, don't just sell them

The product-plus-services (Alternative Legal Service Provider) category is ~$24.5B globally and growing fast. Hourly billing is still the largest share (~59% in 2024) but flat-fee billing is the fastest-growing segment (9.1% CAGR) — the market is migrating from hours to fixed/subscription. Managed-services providers "run the workflow on their own platform with their own talent" — exactly HaTi's shape.

Apply it

Bundle blocks of Advice Desk hours into the software tiers (e.g. Team includes 2 hrs/quarter, Business 6 hrs/quarter, Enterprise a monthly review). Sell flat-fee managed workflows ("we run your NDA / renewal pipeline") — the fastest-growing billing mode. Reserve the full KES 7,500–10,500/hr rate for genuinely bespoke advisory, the one place hourly still wins. This turns variable, discountable services revenue into predictable ARR and makes the software stickier.

5.5 · The mistakes to refuse

Under-pricing

~2× more common than overpricing and far harder to unwind. African founders anchor to "what people can afford" instead of value delivered. You have room to price 2–3× above instinct and still be a fraction of imported CLM.

USD-only

When the Naira fell 225% against the dollar, USD-priced tools became unaffordable overnight. The shilling did a milder version. Price in KES.

Monthly, card-based, no lock-in

Exposes you to churn on every FX move and every failed card. Annual KES prepay is the fix.

Too many tiers & ad-hoc discounts

More than ~3 tiers paralyses buyers; deal-by-deal discounts destroy the price anchor. Keep 3 tiers and make every discount a named, ruled programme.

06Recommended pricing for HaTi

Synthesizing the three research streams into a concrete, testable model. These numbers are anchors to validate (§07), not final list prices.

6.1 · The pricing architecture (decide these first)

  • Currency: KES, fixed. USD shown only as a secondary reference for multinationals.
  • Billing: Annual prepay is the headline (15–20% cheaper than monthly). Monthly available at a premium for those who insist.
  • Metric: Hybrid — a per-workspace platform fee with included seats + an active-contract allowance, then metered expansion (extra seats, extra contract volume). This lets small firms enter cheaply and expand — the Paystack lesson.
  • Anchor: Value, not features. Price against the cost of one avoided disaster — a missed auto-renewal, an unflagged liability clause, a lapsed contract — at ~5–20% of that value.
  • Collection: M-Pesa Paybill / Pesapal (SME) and bank transfer / invoice (mid-market+). VAT 16% handled explicitly.

6.3 · Productize the Advice Desk

Keep the published rate card (KES 7,500–10,500/hr, +25% priority) — but layer three revenue modes on top so services stop being purely variable:

  1. Bundled hours inside each paid tier (above). A block of 2 hrs/quarter is worth ~KES 15,000–21,000 — on its own it makes the Team tier compelling and raises perceived value well above the KES 18k price.
  2. Flat-fee managed workflows — "we run your NDA / renewal / onboarding pipeline for KES X/month." This is the fastest-growing legal-services billing mode and converts hours into recurring revenue.
  3. Bespoke advisory at full hourly rate — complex negotiation and one-off drafting, where hourly still wins and margins are highest.

07The validation research plan

This report's numbers are triangulated anchors. Before printing them, run this five-phase plan to convert desk research into prices proven on real Kenyan buyers. It is sequenced so each phase de-risks the next; the whole thing is runnable in ~10–12 weeks.

PHASE 0 · COMPLETE

Desk research & anchors

This report: local WTP signals, competitor teardown, tax/payment constraints, and a value-based model. Output: the tier anchors in §06 and the open questions below.

PHASE 1 · WEEKS 1–3 · QUALITATIVE

Willingness-to-pay interviews

Who: 15–20 decision-makers across the archetype segments — in-house counsel / GCs, procurement & finance heads at mid-market firms, and managing partners at law firms.

Method: jobs-to-be-done + budget-discovery interviews. What contract pain costs them today; who holds the budget; what they pay for adjacent tools; reaction to shillings vs USD and annual vs monthly. Show the tier concepts, watch for the "that's expensive / that's cheap" line.

Answers: the TAM gap (how many firms have a legal/procurement function), the real buyer, and whether the metric (contracts + seats) matches how they think about value.

PHASE 2 · WEEKS 3–6 · QUANTITATIVE

Price-sensitivity survey

Who: 100–150+ target-segment respondents (recruit via LSK, KEPSA, industry associations, and the interview snowball).

Method: Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity Meter to find the acceptable price band and the point of marginal expensiveness, plus Gabor-Granger to test the specific KES anchors (18k / 45k) and read the demand curve and revenue-maximising point. Segment results by company size and sector.

Answers: whether the anchors sit inside the acceptable band, and where the revenue-maximising price actually is per segment.

PHASE 3 · WEEKS 5–10 · IN-MARKET

Design-partner cohort

What: sign 5–10 paying design partners at a locked founder rate in exchange for a case study, a reference call, and structured feedback. Instrument everything — activation, active-contract growth, which features gate upgrades, and Advice-Desk-hours consumption.

Answers: real willingness-to-pay (not just to say), the true expansion curve for the usage metric, and whether the bundled-hours mechanic drives stickiness. This is also your first ARR and your proof for pricing higher.

PHASE 4 · WEEKS 8–12 · LIVE TEST

Price experiments & paid pilots

What: A/B the published price on landing pages / quotes across segments; offer paid pilots rather than open free trials to filter intent; test annual-vs-monthly take-up and the M-Pesa/Pesapal vs invoice collection paths.

Measure: quote→close conversion, realised ACV, discount leakage, and early churn/renewal signals. Watch that annual prepay adoption is high enough to neutralise card-renewal churn.

PHASE 5 · ONGOING · INSTITUTIONALISE

Repricing & monitoring cadence

Set an annual repricing review (FX buffer, inflation, competitor moves); track net revenue retention as the north-star for the land-and-expand model; monitor the KES/USD rate against any USD cost lines; and keep discounts inside the ruled programmes. Re-run a light Gabor-Granger annually.

08Risks & watch-items

  • Under-pricing drift. The strongest gravitational pull on a local founder. Hold the line at value-based anchors; the research explicitly shows room to price up.
  • Card-renewal churn. If monthly card billing creeps in, silent payment-failure churn follows. Keep annual + M-Pesa/invoice as the default.
  • FX on USD cost lines. Cloud/AI costs are USD; shilling weakness compresses margin even while buyer prices stay fixed. Buffer it into the annual price and review yearly.
  • Advice Desk capacity. Bundled hours are a real delivery liability. Cap and track consumption; price the bundles so they stay margin-positive at expected utilisation.
  • Thin data. No Kenya WTP survey and a dated SME count mean the anchors are inferred — Phases 1–2 must run before the prices are published, not after.
  • Competitor response. A local rival (or an imported vendor localising to KES) could set the anchor first. Speed to a clearly-priced offer is itself strategic — the market currently has no transparent CLM price reference.
